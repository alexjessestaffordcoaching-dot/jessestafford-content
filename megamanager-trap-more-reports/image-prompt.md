## Shared Elements

**Core situation:** A manager's 1-on-1 schedule is full, but the real conversations — the career discussions, the early warnings, the feedback that only surfaces in private — have stopped happening. The meeting is there. The relationship isn't.

**Context box text:** THE MEETING STAYED. THE CONVERSATION DIDN'T.

**Emotional tone:** Direct and focused. Jesse's expression is calm, clear-eyed, and slightly weighted — the expression of someone who sees a problem he helped create. Not dramatic. Not distressed. Just honest.

---

## Concept 1 — The System

**Visual metaphor:** A telephone operator's switchboard — a large panel of connection ports and cords. Some ports are lit and have live, taut connections. Most are dark, with plugged-in cords lying slack and loose.

**First-second read:** A communication board with most connections dark — the manager is present, but most relationships are not live.

**Compositional approach:** Wide shot. The switchboard fills the right two-thirds of the frame, floor-to-ceiling in scale. Jesse stands small in the lower-left third of the frame, looking toward the board. His size relative to the board communicates that the system has grown beyond his reach. The few lit connections draw the eye. The many dark ones tell the story.

### Full Expanded Prompt

COMPOSITION — most important:
Wide shot, 16:9 single panel. A large telephone operator's switchboard fills the right two-thirds of the frame, floor-to-ceiling in scale. The board has dozens of connection ports arranged in a grid. Five or six ports have bright, taut connection cords running from them — active, alive. The remaining ports are dark, with cords plugged in but lying slack and loose against the board face. Jesse stands in the lower-left third of the frame, small relative to the board. He looks toward it with a calm, clear-eyed expression. He is not reaching for any connection. He is looking at the pattern.

Art style spec:
Crisp, uniform charcoal ink outlines (#333333), medium line weight throughout. Comic book precision — not sterile flat-art, not heavy superhero cross-hatching. Lines should suggest a technical patent drawing or a well-crafted indie comic panel. Loose, organic watercolor fills — visibly hand-painted. Color intentionally bleeds slightly outside the ink lines or leaves small white gaps at boundaries. Wet-edge textures and transparency variations are required — these are features, not errors. No digital gradients. No glow effects. No perfectly aligned fills. Background: clean white paper texture (#ffffff) with steampunk mechanical elements — objects have exposed mechanics — rendered in charcoal linework as abstract environmental texture. Mechanical background elements suggest systems and structure without being literal or dominant. 16:9 aspect ratio, single panel.

Color palette:
Primary watercolor wash: #100299 (deep blue). Canvas and negative space: #ffffff. Linework and shadows: #333333. Shadows are a semi-transparent charcoal watercolor wash — never solid black fill. The active connection cords on the board may use warm amber or golden-yellow tones as a supporting color to contrast the dark ports — these should feel warm and alive against the predominantly dark board.

Primary character:
Man in his early 40s. Short buzz-cut hair. Neatly trimmed short beard and mustache. Brown eyes. V-neck long sleeve shirt in deep blue (#100299) watercolor wash. Charcoal pants. Relaxed, unposed. Standing in the lower-left of the frame, looking toward the switchboard. Calm, clear-eyed expression — the look of someone who has recognized a pattern.

Scene description:
The switchboard is the dominant visual element, rendered with patent-drawing precision — each port labeled, the grid showing the full scale of the system. The five or six live connections are visibly bright, the cords taut and in focus. The remaining connections are dim — ports occupied but cords lying flat, without tension. Jesse stands at the left edge of the frame, one hand at his side. The mechanical background behind him reinforces the sense of organizational structure — gears, pipes, routing diagrams rendered softly in charcoal. Negative space is intentional on the left side of the panel.

Context box:
Uppercase sans-serif text inside a solid white (#ffffff) rectangle with a sharp charcoal (#333333) border. Placed at the extreme bottom of the panel. Text: THE MEETING STAYED. THE CONVERSATION DIDN'T.

Tone guardrails:
This image should feel like two people talking through a real problem in plain language. Not a motivational poster. Not corporate (no handshakes, whiteboards, or team huddles). Not inspirational abstraction. Grounded, human, and specific. The mechanical background supports the scene — it does not compete with it.

## Concept 2 — The Room

**Scene dynamic:** Jesse and one other person sitting at a 1-on-1 table, but the table is unexpectedly long — a conference table used for a two-person meeting. Both people look at papers or a device between them rather than at each other. The physical distance between them, and their mutual focus on the document rather than the person, communicates the hollowing of the real conversation.

**First-second read:** Two people at opposite ends of a table that's too long for a 1-on-1, not looking at each other.

**Compositional approach:** Medium shot, slightly wide. The long table runs horizontally across the frame. Jesse sits on the left, the other person on the right, with too much table between them. A document or device sits in the center. Both look at it. Their body language is functional, not relational. The empty middle of the table is the compositional weight.

Both look at it — not at each other. The gap between them, and their parallel focus on the object rather than the person, is the scene. Equal scale between the two figures.

## Concept 3 — The Reframe

**Unexpected angle:** The absent element. The 1-on-1 setup is perfect — two chairs facing each other, small table between them, a notebook open. But one chair has been pushed back at an angle. The seat is still warm (a jacket draped over it). Jesse stands nearby, looking at the empty chair. The 1-on-1 is on the calendar. The person — the relationship — has already left.

**First-second read:** A perfectly staged 1-on-1 setup with one chair empty and pushed back, Jesse looking at it.

**Why it works:** The empty chair is not the person's absence from the meeting. It's the absence of the real conversation within the meeting. The 1-on-1 still happened. Something was left behind. The viewer gets this in about three seconds. It makes the concept land harder than any other framing.

### Full Expanded Prompt

COMPOSITION — most important:
Medium shot, 16:9 single panel. Two chairs face each other with a small table between them — the classic 1-on-1 setup. Jesse's chair is on the left, occupied. He sits forward slightly, elbows on knees, looking at the chair across from him. The second chair is pushed back at an angle — recently occupied, now empty. A jacket is draped over the back of the empty chair. A notebook sits open on the small table between them, with notes from a meeting that clearly happened. The contrast between the complete physical setup and the empty chair is the entire scene. Jesse is central and mid-scale. The empty chair is slightly larger in the frame, drawing the eye.
