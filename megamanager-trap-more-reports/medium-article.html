Quick Bridge: If your headcount grew by 50% or more in the last year and your 1-on-1s have quietly become monthly, you are not looking at a calendar problem. You are looking at a leadership philosophy question. This post is about the first thing that disappears when span grows, why most managers do not notice until it is already gone, and what to decide at the philosophy level before you touch your calendar.

---

What Happens to Your 1-on-1s When Your Team Doubles?

Through my career I've had as few as four direct reports and as many as twenty-eight.

I want to be honest about what happened at twenty-eight. My preference, the one I still hold, is a thirty-minute 1:1 every two weeks with everyone on my team. At twenty-eight directs, that became thirty minutes every month. I created a workaround: a smaller inner group of people who each tracked a different area and brought me what I needed to know.

Most of those inner group people are managers and directors now, so I guess something worked out.

What I am less proud of is how long it took me to see what had happened. The 1-on-1 meetings were still on the calendar. The conversations had already changed.

---

What the Schedule Does Not Show

Gallup's latest workplace data puts the average American manager at 12.1 direct reports today, up from 8.2 in 2013. That's nearly a 50% increase in twelve years. Fortune named it the "megamanager era" this spring, covering how companies have spent three years flattening their middle layers and leaving survivors with dramatically larger portfolios of people.

The coverage focuses on the organizational causes: AI cost-cutting, efficiency pressure, headcount rationalization. All real.

What the coverage does not address is what happens inside the 1-on-1 when span doubles.

The first thing that quietly disappears is not the meeting. It's the real conversation.

The meeting stays on the calendar for a while. What goes first is the career discussion you were having every other week. The early-stage problem the person wasn't going to raise in a standup. The feedback that only surfaces when someone believes you actually have time for them.

Those conversations don't happen when your head is already somewhere else. When you're managing twenty people and mentally working down a checklist of who needs what, you are not present for what doesn't have an agenda item. The meeting fills with status. Status has other channels.

At that point, the 1-on-1 is doing the wrong job.

---

The Philosophy Gap

When span doubles, two things can happen. I've been on both sides of this.

The first group starts optimizing the 1-on-1 for efficiency: shorter, less frequent, more structured. They build templates so they don't forget anything. At some point monthly starts to feel fine. Quarterly starts to feel like enough.

Nothing dramatic happens. The 1-on-1s just quietly stop being the thing they were.

The second group does something different. They do not make the 1-on-1 more efficient. They cut other things to protect it.

They cancel the prep time on meetings that don't require their attention. They drop the recurring syncs nobody would miss. The 1-on-1 frequency stays.

When the 1-on-1 is the mechanism by which you actually lead, you restructure everything else to protect it.

The difference between these two groups is not time management. It's the belief underneath the calendar. Is the 1-on-1 a scheduled obligation? Or is it the primary place where you actually lead?

Leaders who answer the second way find a path. Leaders who answer the first way are already losing it, usually before they realize it's happening.

---

What to Decide Before You Touch Your Calendar

If your team grew recently and your 1-on-1s are under pressure, here is what to work through first.

Decide what the 1-on-1 is for. If yours are carrying status updates, you already have a problem. Status has other channels: standup, Slack, the weekly written update.

The 1-on-1 is for the things that have no other channel: career goals, the relationship, the feedback the person will never raise in a group setting. If you can replace your 1-on-1 with a Slack message, you are not having a real 1-on-1.

Something has to give when span grows. The right question is not what 1-on-1s can become. The question is what else on your calendar is not load-bearing.

Look at the meetings you attend as a stakeholder but do not drive. The recurring syncs scheduled a year ago that nobody would miss. Start cutting those. Your 1-on-1 should be the last thing you move.

Here is what worked when I had twenty-eight. A smaller group of three to five people, each tracking a different area, extended my reach without replacing my direct relationship with the full team. I still held 1-on-1s with everyone. But this group got more of my time and attention.

They became the depth layer. The rest of my team got consistency.

The inner group model is not a workaround. It is succession planning happening in real time. The people I worked with most closely at twenty-eight directs became the leaders who grew from that team.

Name your floor. Set a minimum you will not cross: monthly with everyone, bi-weekly with your most critical people. Then protect it the same way you would protect a customer deadline.

A dropped 1-on-1 is a missed commitment. Reschedule it that week, not the next one.

---

Monday Action

Look at your 1-on-1 calendar. If your team grew in the last six months, count how many moved from bi-weekly to monthly, or quietly disappeared. That is your measurement.

Then ask: was that a scheduling decision or a philosophy decision? Most of the time it is a philosophy decision made by default, without noticing it was being made. That is the one to revisit before anything else.
