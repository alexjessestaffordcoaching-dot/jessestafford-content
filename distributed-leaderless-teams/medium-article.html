This post breaks down the three conditions that make distributed ownership work, drawn from the year I had 28 direct reports and had to rebuild how decisions got made.

---

How to Give Your Team Real Ownership Without Everything Falling Apart

At 28 direct reports, I had a title problem.

I had the org chart. I had the headcount. What I did not have was any way to know what was happening across all of it, give people what they needed, or make good decisions fast enough for the work to move.

I could not do 1-on-1s with everyone at any useful frequency. I could not hold 28 different threads in my head. I could not be the decision point for everything that needed a decision. So I stopped trying to be.

I identified a smaller group. Maybe five or six people who would each keep track of specific areas for me. Not formal leadership titles. Not additional pay. Just clear accountability for what was happening in their area. I worked closely with them.

They surfaced what I needed to know. They made the calls that fell within their scope. I also had to rethink how I tracked my own work. Not everything that came to me needed to.

It took some time to figure out. Most of those people are managers and directors now, so I guess something worked out.

What "Distributed" Actually Means

There is a version of distributed leadership that gets sold as freedom.

Give people ownership and step back. Trust your team. Stop micromanaging.

That version sounds right. It fails regularly.

When leaders hand over ownership without building the conditions for it, people do not take the ball and run. They look around for who is supposed to make this decision. They escalate things that should not be escalated. They stop surfacing problems because they are not sure if it is their problem to surface.

The result looks like an initiative gap. The real problem is that no system was built.

Distributed leadership is not an attitude. It is an architecture.

Three things have to be in place before it works.

---

Condition 1: Scope, Not Just Responsibility

"You are responsible for team quality" is not ownership. It is a category.

Ownership means specific decision authority. These are the decisions you make without me. These are the things you track. When something in this area needs attention, you are the one who notices and acts.

The conversation most leaders skip is not about ownership. It is about limits. Here is what you own. Here is where your authority stops. Here is what escalation looks like.

The guardrail also needs to run in both directions. Not just what they own, but what they do not own. What requires you. Where the line is. Without that, they either over-escalate because they are unsure, or they make calls that should have come to you because nobody drew the boundary.

If your direct report is not sure whether a decision is theirs to make, that boundary was never drawn. They are going to default to asking. Not because they lack initiative. Because the system was not designed.

The test: can the person describe their scope to someone else without your input? If not, it is not ownership yet.

---

Condition 2: Context, Not Just Process

Context is what enables judgment.

Process tells people what to do. Context tells them why. And it is the why that lets them adapt when the situation changes.

A person following a process freezes when circumstances stop matching the steps. A person who understands what outcome the process is protecting can make a reasonable call when something unexpected happens. That is not a special skill. That is context doing its job.

Most leaders explain the how and skip the why. They hand off a solid process and assume the team can figure out the rest. When the team defaults to asking, that is not a sign of low initiative. That is a context gap.

Before you hand anything off: tell them why this work matters. Not the steps. The outcome the steps are protecting. What does a good call look like when the steps do not fit? When they can answer that, the context transfer is real.

---

Condition 3: Repeat the Goals

This gets treated as a one-time handoff. "Here is what we are working toward. Go."

That is not how people internalize what matters. There is an old principle that people need to hear something seven times before it shapes their behavior. The part that gets left out: it is seven times per person. Ten people means seventy touchpoints for one goal.

The leader's job is not to announce goals. It is to keep them alive. In every 1-on-1. In every team meeting. In the moments when something unexpected happens and someone has to decide without you. When the goal is alive in the room, people make decisions that serve it. When it was said once and never again, they make decisions based on what is directly in front of them.

---

The Communication Gap That Shows Up at Scale

Here is what happens when a team grows faster than the system.

A leader builds a smaller team. They handle most of the coordination work themselves. Visibility, relationships, tracking what the rest of the organization cares about. The team delivers well.

But nobody ever learns why that work matters or how it connects to anything outside their immediate scope.

Then the leader's responsibilities expand. They no longer have the bandwidth to do what they had been doing. The work quietly stops. Nobody steps in. Nobody even notices it is missing until months of organizational drift have already happened.

This is not a motivation problem or a talent problem. It is a communication gap that existed before the growth. The growth just made it visible.

The question to ask yourself: if you stepped back for three months, what work would quietly stop that your team does not know is important?

That is what to start transferring now. Not because you are leaving. Because it should not live only with you.

---

What This Looks Like Monday Morning

Three things to make explicit.

Name the guardrails, not just the responsibility. Pick one area where you are currently making most of the calls. Identify one person who could own it. Write down what ownership actually means: what decisions fall inside their scope, what requires escalation, what success looks like. Have that conversation.

Give context before you hand off. Before asking someone to take ownership of something, tell them why the work matters. Not the process. The outcome it protects. What goes wrong when it disappears.

Repeat your goals more than feels necessary. In every 1-on-1 this week, say out loud what the team is working toward. It will feel redundant. That is the point.

The leaders who get this right are not the ones who trust more. They are the ones who build the conditions first, then get out of the way.
