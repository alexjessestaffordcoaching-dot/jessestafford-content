I once had 28 direct reports. I could not lead any of them well.

Not because the team lacked talent. Because I had never built anything behind me. Every decision ran through me. Every escalation came to me. When my bandwidth disappeared, everything that had been depending on it went with it.

Here is what I see leaders get wrong about distributed ownership: they think it is an attitude. Trust your team. Step back. Let people lead. They hand off responsibility without scope, context, or repeated goals. Then they wonder why the team keeps waiting for direction.

Distributed leadership is not a trust exercise. It is a design exercise.

If your team keeps escalating decisions you think they should handle, the missing piece is usually one of three things: they do not know what they specifically own, they do not have the context to make the call you would make, or the goal has not been said enough times to shape their behavior. Any one of those gaps will pull you back to the center.

What would you say is the hardest part of actually letting your team own something?
