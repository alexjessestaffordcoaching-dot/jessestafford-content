Title: You Keep Getting Outvoted in Meetings. Here's What's Actually Happening.

---

I had a direct report who had visible negative reactions to change initiatives, or surprise information that affected their team.

When something significant came up, the reaction would surface immediately. Right there in the room, in front of whoever was there. It was creating friction for the team.

My solution was simple. I started sharing things with them in advance. Before the team meeting, before the all-hands, before anything formal. I would walk through the news with them one-on-one, give them space to process it while it was just the two of us.

By the time we were in the room together, they had already worked through it. The reaction was already spent.

It worked every time. That solved the team problem. But the reason it worked is what I have been thinking about ever since.

---

The Meeting Is Not Where the Decision Happens

Most leaders believe the meeting is where persuasion takes place. You build a solid deck. You rehearse your points. You show up with data and logic and a clear recommendation.

Then you get outvoted.

Not because your argument was wrong. Because the people in the room had already formed their positions before the agenda was sent. The leaders who left with a yes did not win the meeting. They won the days before it.

Formal meetings are where decisions get announced. The decision-making already happened somewhere else.

I see this situation come up often. The one who built the best deck walks out confused. The one who spent the previous days in quiet conversations walks out with the yes.

McKinsey put a number on it: 61% of executives say at least half the time they spend making decisions in meetings is wasted. Their researchers found that real strategies are not made in conference rooms. They are shaped beforehand, in smaller conversations, before the agenda was ever sent. The formal meeting is where the decision gets announced.

Everyone walked in with their minds already made up.

---

What Leaders Who Get the Yes Are Actually Doing

Watch the people in your organization who consistently get what they want in formal settings. Not the most senior ones by title. The ones who seem to get their projects greenlit, their ideas funded, their recommendations adopted.

They are not better presenters than you.

They are doing something in the days, weeks, and months before the meeting that most leaders don't recognize or believe isn't necessary. They find out who will push back and have that conversation first. The objections get surfaced privately, one-on-one, before the formal setting makes them impossible to reverse.

By the time they walk into the room, the hard work is already done.

I started doing this with a colleague who was skeptical of almost everything I proposed. Before any important decision, I would call them first. Not to lobby them. To understand what they were actually worried about.

Half the time, I found out the concern was something I could address. The other half, I found out their concern was not really about my idea at all. Something else was going on. Either way, I walked into the meeting knowing exactly where the conversation was going.

Getting things done at this level is about relationships across the company. The leaders who have those relationships already built are not the ones walking out of meetings asking what just happened.

---

A Private "No" Is a Conversation. A Public "No" Is a Vote.

In a formal meeting, a public objection is almost impossible to reverse. The person who objected has an audience. Walking it back looks like caving. They defend their position, others align behind them, and the meeting ends with "let's take more time to discuss this."

Nothing moves.

The same conversation, one-on-one, two days earlier? That is a conversation. You can hear the real concern without anyone watching.

They do not have to defend a position in front of the group. The conversation goes places a formal meeting never will.

Most decisions get shaped in the informal conversations that most leaders are not having.

---

How to Do the Pre-Meeting Work

This is not about lobbying. It is not about building a coalition against someone else's position. It is about doing the honest work of understanding where the resistance is before the meeting starts.

Here is what it looks like in practice.

Map who matters before you map your slides. Before you build the deck, identify the two or three people whose opinions will shape the outcome. Not just the decision-maker. The person they listen to, the first one to object, the peer who tried something similar and failed.

Call the person most likely to push back. Not to convince them. To understand their concern. Ask what they are worried about.

Ask what it would take for them to say yes. Then listen.

Five minutes on the phone surfaces more than ten emails. Text loses context. A real conversation closes the gaps that a well-formatted slide never will.

Share context, not arguments. You are not trying to win the argument before the meeting. You are trying to make sure the people who matter have what they need.

There is a real difference between "here is why you should vote yes" and "here is the background I thought you'd want before we meet." One puts people on the defensive. The other does not.

Surface the objection before the room does. If someone is going to say no, you want that conversation to happen before everyone is watching. Find out what would have to change for them to say yes.

Sometimes the answer shifts your approach entirely. Sometimes it tells you the real concern is something else entirely. The meeting would never have surfaced it.

The trust you need for this conversation does not come from the pre-meeting itself. It comes from the relationships you built before you needed them. You already know the people whose opinions matter and you already have the trust to pick up the phone. (More on this: Jesse Stafford on Medium)

---

Monday Action

Before your next important meeting, identify the one person most likely to push back on the decision. Call them before the meeting. Not to argue. To understand what they are thinking.

Notice what changes in the room when you walk in having already had that conversation.

That is not a meeting trick. It is a different way of working.
