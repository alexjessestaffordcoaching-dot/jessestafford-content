Quick Bridge: Most directors get promoted for solving problems fast. Once you are managing managers, that same instinct becomes the bottleneck. Here is the distinction between describing a problem and solving one, with the exact questions to ask when something lands on your desk, so you stop being the answer key and start operating at the level you were promoted to.

---

You got promoted because you solved problems. Here is why that job does not exist at director level.

When my responsibilities expanded and I got promoted to director, I did not slow down. I just kept doing what had worked.

I kept grooming backlogs. I kept meeting with internal customers. I kept doing the internal marketing that made my team visible. The work kept moving, and that felt like the right signal.

Then the floor dropped out.

I no longer had room in my schedule for everything my original team needed, plus everything the expanded role required. The work I had been doing for them just stopped happening. Nobody picked it up.

Nobody knew why it mattered enough to make it a priority. The team fell into a mild obscurity that took years to climb out of.

Not because they were not capable. Because I had never described what I was doing or why it mattered. I had just done it. And when I could no longer do it, there was nothing for them to carry forward.

That was the moment I understood that the job had changed and I had not.

The trap that catches most new directors

Here is what nobody tells you when you move into this role.

You got to where you are because you solve problems well. That skill is real. It works. It kept getting rewarded, which means you kept leaning on it.

By the time you reach director, solving problems is not just something you do. It is how you think about your value.

The trap: the job has changed. You have not.

You are now managing managers. You have more teams, more stakeholders, more competing demands than any single person can process. The number of problems coming at you scales faster than your hours do.

When you keep solving, you become the bottleneck.

Your managers wait for your answer before they act. Your teams stop developing judgment because you keep making the calls. Decisions that should take a day take a week because they cannot move without you. You are exhausted because you are doing their job while also trying to do yours.

This is not a time management problem. You cannot solve a time management problem by getting more efficient. Solving got you promoted. It is also what will stall you here.

---

What the job actually requires

Here is the frame that changed how I operate: you are a problem describer now, not a problem solver.

Your value is not in solving the problems that reach you. It is in describing those problems clearly enough that the right people can solve them.

That sounds passive. It is the opposite.

Most problems that land on a director are not clean. They are ambiguous, they span multiple teams, and they require trade-offs nobody wants to make explicitly. The instinct is to take the problem, work it yourself, hand back the answer. That instinct is fast and it feels helpful.

But when you hand back the answer, your team never learns to sit with the ambiguity. They never build the judgment to make the trade-off. Every problem that reaches you creates one more thing they rely on you for. Over time, you have built a team that stops when you do.

When you describe the problem instead of solving it, you are doing something harder and more valuable. You are naming the situation clearly enough that someone else can pick it up. That is the work that lets the right person make the right call without you in the room.

---

What describing a problem actually looks like

This is not a philosophy. It is a practice. Here is how it works.

Before you say anything, write out what you see. "Here is the situation as I understand it. Here is what is at stake. Here is what I do not know yet." That act alone separates the signal from the noise. Most problems feel large until you put them into words. Once you write it down, you can often see which part is the real problem and which part is anxiety about the problem.

Name who owns it before you touch it. "This is yours to solve. I want to hear your thinking before we decide anything."

You may have a strong view on the answer. That view is not the point. Their judgment is the point.

When you name ownership first, you are telling them the problem belongs to them. When you hand them an answer, you are telling them you open the outcome.

When your direct reports bring you a problem, ask them to describe it first. "Tell me what you see as the problem." Not because you do not have a view. Because if they cannot describe the problem, they cannot solve it.

Their description tells you exactly where they are stuck. That is where your help actually matters.

The test I use: if I can describe a problem clearly enough that someone else could act on it without me in the room, I have done the work. If they still need me to solve it, I have not.

---

The moment this shifts for leaders

A client I worked with was a director who kept saying his managers could not think at the right level. He was constantly stepping in to make calls they should have been making themselves.

When I asked him to walk me through the last time a problem came to him, the pattern became visible immediately. His manager had described a situation. He had immediately started working the problem.

He gave his manager the answer. His manager left to execute.

His managers were not thinking at the wrong level. They were not thinking at all. He was describing the answer so quickly that there was no space for them to describe the problem. He had removed the step that builds judgment.

The shift was small. When a manager came to him with a problem, he started saying: "Tell me what you see. What do you think is actually going on here?"

He made them describe it first. Then he added what he saw that they might be missing. Then they named who was going to own the solution.

His managers started making better calls within a month. Not because they had changed. Because they finally had to use the part of their brain they had been outsourcing to him.

Monday action

The next time a problem lands on your desk this week, do not solve it.

Before you say anything, ask: "Walk me through what you see as the problem." Let them describe it fully. Then add: "Here is what I see that you might be missing." Then ask: "Who do you think should own this?"

That is the whole move. You are there to sharpen the picture, not paint it yourself. Do it once this week. Notice who starts solving without waiting for you.
