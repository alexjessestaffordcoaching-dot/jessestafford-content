Why Are Software Engineers So Hard to Work With?

Quick Bridge: If you manage software engineers, you probably already feel it. The overconfidence about their own work paired with skepticism about everyone else's. The debates that go in circles without landing on a decision. The principled resistance to doing the things that would actually advance their careers. This post names all three patterns, explains what is actually driving them, and gives you one specific thing to say Monday morning that starts to change things.

---

I have been managing software engineers for over twenty years. I have built my career alongside them. Some of my best professional relationships are with engineers.

And I have a list of the things I genuinely hate about working with them.

I rarely say this out loud. The engineering managers I talk to rarely say it either.

The frustrations are not edge cases. They are patterns. And if you manage engineers, you already know exactly what I am about to describe.

---

The Three Patterns That Make Engineers Hard to Lead

The first is this: complete pessimism about other engineers' work, while simultaneously being over-optimistic about their own.

I have watched this long enough to recognize it as a pattern, not a coincidence. The engineer who dismisses a colleague's design as poorly thought through is often the same engineer who is completely confident their own estimate is accurate. Both are usually wrong.

It turns out there is actually a name for this. Dunning-Kruger.

Engineers overestimate their own work while underestimating the people around them. Even the genuinely skilled ones. You cannot accurately evaluate someone else's problem from the outside.

The practical result: code reviews become status contests. Estimates become turf battles. Planning meetings become arguments where everyone is defending the same position from a different angle.

The second pattern is the never-ending discussion about semantics.

Engineering culture rewards precision. That is a real strength. The ability to think in exact terms, to catch definitional errors, to notice when two people are using the same word to mean different things. This matters enormously when you are writing code.

The problem is when that same instinct shows up in a planning meeting where the goal is a decision. I have sat in rooms where the first thirty minutes went to debating what "done" means for a feature no one has started building yet. The precision instinct is valuable. The context is the problem.

The third pattern is the one that bothers me most, because I have watched it cost people careers.

Engineers who take an idealistic stance against the things that would actually help them advance.

Not ethical objections. Principled resistance to visibility work.

"I should not have to talk in meetings. They should pay attention to me and know what I am doing."

"I should not need to work with that other team to get the right result. Where is their product manager?"

I watched a game engineer notice a project was running behind and go directly to the project manager with this: "You need to move the date by two weeks because we are late." The PM pushed back and started asking questions. The engineer felt disrespected. Their honest assessment was not being accepted.

What the engineer did not see was how the message landed. A directive is received differently than information. Had they said "Things are running behind and I need two more weeks to get back on track" the reaction would have been completely different.

Same situation. Different framing. Completely different outcome.

The engineer was not wrong about the facts. They were operating on a principle that was hurting them in practice.

An engineer I had on one of my teams spent three years doing excellent work that almost no one saw. When the promotion stalled, they were genuinely confused. They had done everything right. They had built things that worked.

They had not made their impact visible to the people who make promotion decisions. They believed the work would speak for itself. At the size their organization had become, the work could not.

---

Why These Behaviors Exist

Here is the part that took me years to understand.

These patterns are not personality defects. They are the predictable output of how engineers are trained, hired, and rewarded.

Engineers spend their entire education focused on one thing: find the best solution. The person who does that fastest and cleanest wins. You build confidence by being right. You advance by knowing more than the people around you.

The overconfidence and the semantic precision are not accidents. They are what the system selected for.

Engineers are also taught early that if you build good things, people will notice. That was true when the team was small and everyone could see the work directly. It stops being true somewhere around the time the organization hits twenty or thirty people, and nobody tells the engineer that the rules changed.

When I watch an engineer resist the visibility work that would directly lead to the promotion they feel they deserve, I am not watching stubbornness. I am watching someone who has not yet been given an honest account of how the game actually works at the level they want to reach.

---

What Changes When It Works

I have managed engineers who were all three of these things. Skeptical of everyone's work but their own. Precise to a fault in the wrong moments. Principled in ways that were quietly damaging their career.

Some of them are now directors and senior managers.

What changed was not their technical skill. That was never the problem.

What changed was that someone told them the truth about how their behavior was landing. Not as a criticism. As information they did not have.

One of them needed to see how their work was landing with the people responsible for their career. Another needed to hear that precision is a strength in a code review and a liability in a planning meeting. What they all had in common was the same missing piece: honest information about how they were being seen.

When engineers understand what is actually required to succeed at the level they are trying to reach, most of them adjust. They are problem-solvers by nature. Give them the right problem and they will work on it.

The frustrations I named at the start of this post are worth naming. Not to vent. To diagnose.

If you are frustrated by any of these patterns, you are not managing broken people. You are managing people who do not yet have the full picture. That is a different problem. And it has a different solution.

---

Your Monday action: Pick one engineer who is showing one of these three patterns. Not all three. One.

Identify the specific behavior that is limiting them most right now. This week, have a single conversation with them about it. Not as a critique. As information they might not have.

"Here is how this is landing with the people who will influence your career."

That conversation is where the relationship starts to actually change.
