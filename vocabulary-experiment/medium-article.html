What Words Are You Using That Change How Your Team Listens?

Platform: Medium | Target: 1,000-1,500 words | Schedule: 2026-05-06 (Tuesday) Pillar: 1-on-1s & Communication Status: FINAL — Pending Jesse's approval

---

Quick Bridge: If your team responses feel flat or transactional, the problem might not be what you are saying. It might be the words you are automatically choosing to say it. This post gives you a four-step observation system Jesse ran for several weeks: watch other people's word patterns, then watch your own, then choose one phrase to change, and build from there. The shift is small. The impact on how people hear you is not.

---

Opening

I wanted to change my language to be more positive. I started by spending several weeks listening to other people's word choices and noting the impact of positive and negative words. Next, I expanded my focus to my own word choices, noting when I chose to use negative words when a positive one would have served the same purpose. As I did this I naturally started choosing different words when I spoke.

The turning point came with a specific example. I would say "I need to go argue with the helpdesk." For months. Every time something needed their attention, I was going to "argue."

So I changed it. "I'm going to have a conversation with the helpdesk." The shift was small. The task was identical.

But something changed in how I showed up for that conversation. And something changed in how the helpdesk responded to me.

---

What I Actually Noticed

When I started this experiment, I expected the shift to feel like editing myself. Choose better words, sound more polished. That is not what happened.

I noticed the team responded differently to the same situations. Same problem, different word from me, different energy in the room. It was not about professionalism. It was about what the word signaled before the conversation even started.

When you tell your team "I need to argue with another department," they hear conflict. They hear tension. They hear a problem that cannot be solved collaboratively.

When you say "I'm going to have a conversation," they hear something different. They hear problem-solving. They hear possibility.

You are not lying in either case. You are describing the same interaction. But the words you choose set the temperature of the room before anyone says another word.

The words you choose create the emotional temperature before the interaction happens.

You are not trying to be more positive. You are choosing more precisely. Every time you speak, you are making a choice. Most of the time, it is not a conscious one.

---

Why This Starts With Listening to Other People

When you first start paying attention to your word choices, it feels awkward. You are consciously choosing words instead of running on autopilot. You are slowing down.

Here is what happens next. You notice other people's word choices. You start to hear how someone's vocabulary shapes the room.

A team member who says "I will try to complete this" signals low confidence. A team member who says "I intend to complete this by Friday" signals commitment. Same person. Same capability.

I started catching myself mid-sentence. I was about to say something, and I noticed the word I was reaching for. I paused. I chose differently.

The person in front of me responded differently. Not because they were somehow more positive. But because I gave them a different frame to work within.

After several weeks of this conscious practice, something shifts. You stop having to think about it. The positive framing becomes your automatic choice.

You are no longer forcing positivity. You are expressing your actual intent more clearly.

---

Three Moves to Start Right Now

1. Listen for 3 Days Before Changing Anything

Do not try to change your vocabulary yet. Just listen. Pay attention to how you talk. Notice the words that carry negative connotations.

Some common ones: "argue," "deal with," "try to," "hopefully," "unfortunately," "problem," "broken," "failed." Write them down.

Notice how other people talk. Who sounds confident? Who sounds tentative? What is the difference in their word choices?

This listening phase is not judgment. It is data gathering. You are training yourself to notice patterns you have been on automatic with for years.

2. Pick One Phrase to Shift

Do not try to rewrite your entire vocabulary. Pick one phrase you use regularly that carries the wrong connotation. If you "argue" with other teams a lot, that is your phrase. If you "deal with" problems constantly, that is the one.

Notice when you are about to use it. Pause. Find a word or phrase that describes the same action but with a different connotation.

"Argue" becomes "collaborate on a solution." "Deal with" becomes "work through" or "resolve."

Speak the new version. Notice how it feels. Notice how people respond.

3. Add One New Phrase Every Two Weeks

You are not trying to transform overnight. You are building a new automatic pattern. Every two weeks, pick another phrase. Repeat the same process.

The steps are the same every time: notice it, pause, choose differently, speak it.

After two months of this, you will have shifted five automatic phrases. After six months, you will have shifted fifteen. Your team will notice. They will not know exactly what changed.

But they will notice something. You are calmer. More intentional. More precise.

Write them down.

Do not change anything yet. Just notice.
