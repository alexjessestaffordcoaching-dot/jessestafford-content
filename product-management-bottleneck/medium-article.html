How to Stay Valuable as an Engineering Leader When AI Handles the Technical Work

Quick Bridge: You built your career on technical depth. AI is compressing the execution work that depth used to unlock. The constraint has shifted from how to build to what to build. Here are four moves for building the judgment and organizational skills that are actually scarce at the senior level now.

---

Something changed in the last few years, and most engineering leaders I know feel it without being able to name it.

I got to a point where I really was not enjoying my work.

I was putting in maybe three or four hours of real effort a day and doing the minimum to get my tasks done. I was executing fine. Nothing was breaking. Nobody was complaining.

And yet I could feel that I was not building toward anything.

I decided something had to change. But I did not want to leave and land somewhere I would be just as stuck. So I sat with a harder question: which specific parts of my work do I actually enjoy?

For me, the answer was analyzing process and mentoring peers. That answer changed everything.

Because I redirected my time toward process and mentoring, I became the expert in how our business developed product beginning to end. Not just my team's work. Every team and department involved. That let me become vocal about where we could improve.

Early on it was about eliminating duplicate efforts. Later it was about applying approaches from other industries. In one year, I made over $10 million in time efficiency improvements across the company.

The promotions came. I did not chase them. The business asked me to move up every time.

The skills that got me there had nothing to do with how fast I could execute.

That experience happened years before AI was part of any conversation. What I figured out then, the industry is now being forced to figure out at scale.

---

What the AI Moment Is Confirming

Andrew Ng put it plainly: AI has made coding approximately 10x faster. The traditional engineering team ratio of six engineers to every one product manager is inverting.

Some teams are now running two product managers for every one engineer. That shift has a name. When building gets cheap, the constraint moves to deciding what to build.

Here is what nobody is saying out loud: if you spent the last 10 to 15 years getting promoted for being great at building, the ground underneath that career path just shifted.

In my experience, technical execution has always had a ceiling as a career strategy. The engineers who break through it are not the ones who code faster or solve harder problems. They are the ones who start asking a different question. Not "how do we build this?" but "what should we build, and why?"

That shift sounds obvious. It is not easy. Most engineering leaders were hired, evaluated, and promoted because of their technical depth. When things feel uncertain, the instinct is to go deeper.

But when building gets compressed by AI, execution depth stops being a differentiator. It becomes a baseline. And baselines do not get you promoted.

The work AI consistently cannot do is synthesis. Understanding why a business needs what it needs. Knowing which technical direction protects an organization's options three years out. Reading a room full of competing priorities and landing on a decision people can actually trust.

A client I worked with recently described it well. I asked what would change about his role if his entire engineering team got 10x faster overnight. He sat with it. Then he said: "I would spend a lot less time explaining things to people, and a lot more time figuring out what we should be explaining in the first place."

That is the job. At every level above senior engineer, that is the job.

---

This Is Not a Warning

This is a career signal.

The most valuable thing I learned when I moved into a director role had nothing to do with engineering. It was learning about other parts of the business and building relationships across departments.

The people who are willing to understand the business beginning to end are best positioned to make a big impact. They bring the most realistic solutions to the table.

They are not threatened by AI taking over execution. They are relieved. Every hour AI takes off their plate is an hour they can redirect to the work that was always supposed to matter most.

---

Four Moves That Build the Scarce Skills

Get curious about adjacent problems.

Most engineering leaders understand their domain deeply and their organization sideways. Flip that ratio. Start asking how other departments make money, lose money, and decide what to build. Sit in on a product planning meeting.

Ask your finance partner what keeps them up at night. You are not trying to become a generalist. You are building context you cannot get from inside your own team.

Take a position on what should be built, not just how to build it.

Engineering leaders who only answer the "how" question are easy to replace. The ones who develop a point of view on "what" and "why" become part of the decisions. That is the price of having an opinion worth asking for.

Train your replacement at the execution level.

Once you can do something quickly, delegate it. The leaders who hold execution close because they are good at it are blocking their own path. What you can do in your sleep is exactly what you should be teaching someone else to do. That frees you for the work the org actually needs from you at your level.

Build relationships before you need them, especially outside your team.

Getting things done at the senior level is about using relationships across the company. You cannot synthesize what you have not been exposed to. The leaders who understand the full business are best positioned to make an impact. You build that understanding deliberately, one relationship and one conversation at a time.

This Week

Pick one decision your organization is wrestling with where you have a technical perspective but have been staying in the "how to build it" lane. Write out your point of view on what should be built and why. Share it in the next meeting where it is relevant.

That is the muscle. Building it starts with one rep.
