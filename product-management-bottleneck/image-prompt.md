## Shared Elements

**Core situation:** An engineering leader looks at the section of his work that requires judgment — deciding what to build — while the execution work behind him runs itself without needing him.

**Context box text:** STOP SOLVING HOW. START OWNING WHAT.

**Emotional tone:** Direct and focused. Jesse's expression is calm and clear-eyed — not anxious, not triumphant. He sees the situation clearly and is choosing to move toward the harder work. No smile. Forward-facing gaze with deliberate composure.

## Concept 1 — The System

**Visual metaphor:** A routing and decision control board with two distinct sections — one automated and running on its own (execution, no human needed), one open and waiting (judgment, requires a person). Jesse stands at the open decision section. The execution machinery hums along without him.

**First-second read:** A man looking toward an empty control panel that is waiting for a decision, while everything to his right runs itself. Viewer reads: the automated part is solved; the human part is still open.

**Compositional approach:** Jesse in the lower-left quarter of the frame, standing, small relative to the board. The execution section fills the right two-thirds — gears, flow indicators, pipes, all moving without anyone present. The open decision section (upper-left/center) has unactivated routing handles and indicators waiting for input. Jesse faces the open section, not the running one.

## Concept 3 — The Reframe

**Unexpected angle:** Scale inversion. Jesse is seated at a workbench, holding a ruler carefully in both hands — focused on it, measuring something with precision. The ruler is large in his grip. Directly behind him on the wall: a full architectural blueprint, detailed and expansive, covering most of the wall. The blueprint dwarfs the ruler in scale and strategic scope — but Jesse has not looked at it yet.

**First-second read:** A man focused on a small precision tool while a large strategic blueprint covers the wall behind him, unread. The scale relationship between the ruler and the blueprint is the entire joke and the entire point.

**Why it works:** The ruler represents execution precision — the thing AI now does faster. The blueprint represents judgment — what to build and why. The irony is immediate: he is measuring carefully while the actual question (what should be built) is right behind him, waiting to be read. The viewer sees the reframe before the context box confirms it.
