Quick Bridge: You were promoted above someone who was once your organizational superior. The relationship gets all the attention. The feedback never does. This post names the two ways that seniority history gets inside your feedback loop and gives you a concrete process to give feedback that is actually about the work.

---

How to Give Feedback When You Manage Someone Who Used to Outrank You

I have managed people who trained me when I started at the company.

People who had been there since before I was born.

One person who had originally interviewed me for a role and decided not to hire me.

The history was different in each case. The feedback problem was the same every time. And I did not catch it until after I had already done some damage.

---

The Problem Is Not the Relationship

Most of the conversation about managing someone who once outranked you focuses on the dynamic. On whether there is tension. On whether they resent the change or question your authority.

Those things matter. They are not where the real damage lives.

The real damage is in the feedback. In what you say and what you leave unsaid across every 1-on-1, every performance conversation, every coaching moment with this person.

Here is how it shows up.

One version is that you soften. You hold back observations you would give to anyone else. You frame things more positively than the work deserves. You let things slide because somewhere underneath it, it still feels uncomfortable to hold accountable someone who once held authority over you.

The other version is that you compensate. Your standard for "good enough" shifts higher with this person specifically. You look harder for what needs fixing in their work. You hold them to a level of scrutiny that does not apply to anyone else on your team.

Both feel like professional judgment. Neither is.

---

Why You Do Not Catch It

Both modes look completely normal from the outside.

Too soft looks like patience. It looks like trust, like giving someone room, like believing in a long arc. Too hard looks like high standards. It looks like accountability, like rigor, like caring about the quality of the work.

The resistance is in your own perception of the situation. The behavior looks like management.

What should make you stop and look is not how any single conversation feels. It is the pattern across all your conversations with this person.

Ask yourself: Is my feedback with this person consistently less specific than what I give everyone else? Are my notes thinner after their 1-on-1s? Do I delay hard conversations with them that I would not delay with anyone else?

Or ask the opposite: Do I look for problems in their work before I look for progress? Have I held them to a standard that does not apply to anyone else? Have I left a feedback conversation feeling something that was not really about the work?

Those are uncomfortable questions. Worth sitting with.

---

Getting Your Feedback Back to Clean

You do not need to address the history with them directly. You do not need to bring it into a 1-on-1. The work happens in your head before you walk into the room.

Three things help.

Name the history to yourself. Out loud. In private. Before the conversation.

Say what it is: this person was once above me in the org chart. That dynamic existed. It ended. What does this person actually need from me today?

You cannot set something aside that you have not first picked up and named.

Anchor feedback in a specific behavior. Not an impression, not a general sense of how things are going. A specific situation, what they did, the impact it had, and what a better response would have looked like.

"In Thursday's team meeting, you redirected a question from your engineer before they had finished asking it. Two people stopped contributing for the rest of the session. Next time, let them finish before you decide how to respond."

When feedback is tied to what actually happened, the history has less surface area to distort it.

Run the equity test before hard conversations. Before any significant feedback conversation with this person, ask yourself one question: would I give this same feedback, at this level of directness, to someone I had no shared history with?

If the answer is no, stop. Find out why. That gap is where the old dynamic is still running.

Most people do not catch this through reflection. They catch it after something specific makes the pattern impossible to miss. The equity test gives you a way to catch it before that moment.

The person you manage deserves feedback that is actually about them.

---

Monday Action

Before your next 1-on-1 with this person, take five minutes alone first.

Name the history out loud: who they used to be in the org chart, who you are now. Then say what you actually think of their work, completely separate from that context.

Walk in with only the second part.
