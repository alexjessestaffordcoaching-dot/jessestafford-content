She sat through the entire strategy meeting without saying a word.

Not because she disagreed. Not because she wasn't paying attention. She was waiting for the moment when she would be certain enough to speak. When she'd have the perfect counterargument all lined up.

By the time that moment came — if it came at all — the decision was already made.

Two weeks later, her peer said, "I figured you were fine with it. You didn't say anything."

She wasn't fine with it. She'd never been fine with it.

Here's what most technical leaders don't realize: in a meeting, your silence doesn't signal thoughtfulness. It signals agreement.

And if you stay silent enough times, people stop asking for your input. They stop routing decisions through you. They assume you've got nothing to add.

The path out isn't being more certain. It's being more visible.

A good question beats a perfect answer. Offering your partial perspective beats waiting for the complete one. Asking the room to stress-test its own thinking beats pushing back with ammunition.

You don't need all the answers to be in the conversation. You just need to show up to it.

When was the last time you stayed silent when you actually had something on your mind?
