## Story Used

**Story 28 — The Manager Who Walked His Team in Circles Because He Hadn't Stopped Being a Peer**

Riley selected this as the primary anchor because it is the inverse of the post's thesis: Jesse had the title and still wasn't choosing to lead. The San Francisco walking-in-circles scene is the opening of the post.

**Why it was selected:** No story in the bank showed Jesse stepping up among more senior people and being followed (the direct version of the thesis). Story 28 is the strongest adjacent story — it shows what happens when the person with the title doesn't decide. The team followed him in circles. The post draws the opposite conclusion: this is what choosing NOT to lead looks like, so here is what choosing to lead actually is.

**Exact text used as the anchor:**
> "We were in San Francisco for a work trip. Five of us. We finished a meeting and headed out to find lunch. They followed me down the street. Through intersections. Around corners. I had no idea where I was going. I walked us in a complete circle. Nobody questioned it. Nobody said 'hey, where are we actually going?' They just followed. That's when it hit me: they were waiting for me to decide. Not because I knew San Francisco. Because I was their manager. And I had been avoiding exactly that for six months."

**Note:** This story appeared in "I'm Managing My Former Peers. Now What?" (Oct 2025) under the peer-to-manager angle. This post uses the same scene for a different lesson: not "how to transition from peer to manager" but "what happens when you have the title and still don't choose to lead." The angles are distinct. See Before Approving item #1.
