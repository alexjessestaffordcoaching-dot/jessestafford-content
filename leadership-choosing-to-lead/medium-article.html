How to Lead Before You Have the Title

Six months into managing my first team, I walked them in a complete circle through downtown San Francisco.

We had finished a meeting and headed out to find lunch. I started down the street with no idea where I was going. We ended up right back where we started.

Nobody questioned it. Nobody said "hey, where are we actually going?" They just followed.

That is when I realized what I had been doing in my actual job for six months. I had the title. I was not choosing to lead.

I had been holding consensus meetings for things that did not require consensus. I softened every call. I waited for more information, more certainty. The team followed me in circles because they were waiting for someone to pick a direction.

I was the manager. I just was not managing.

The moment that changed was a sprint decision. Our business ran on rapid prototyping. Two-week sprints were too slow.

I knew it, made the call, and changed us to one week. The team pushed back hard.

It was rough at first. But it worked. More importantly, I stopped apologizing for making the decision. I owned it.

Leadership did not start when I got the title. It started when I decided.

Most People in That Room Do Not Want to Lead

Here is what most leadership content leaves out.

The framing is usually about you. Whether you are ready. Whether you have enough authority.

Whether they will follow you. The real question is not about you. It is about everyone else.

I have watched this pattern repeat in team after team. Very few people in any given room actually want to decide. A Deloitte survey backs this up: only 6% of Gen Z and millennial professionals say achieving a leadership position is their primary career goal. DDI research found that 71% say taking on a leadership role contributed to their stress levels.

In most rooms, most of the time, most people are quietly hoping someone else will step up and decide.

They do not want the meeting to run long. They do not want to be the person who called the wrong shot. They have their own work to get back to. Leadership carries a burden, and the majority of people are quietly relieved when someone else picks it up.

That is the dynamic in almost every room you walk into. It is not "will they follow me?" It is "does anyone else here actually want this?" The answer is almost never yes.

Once you see that, the room looks completely different. You are not competing for authority. You are filling a vacancy almost everyone else is actively avoiding.

---

The Mechanism: What Creates Followers Is Not the Title

The person who takes the burden of the decision off the room's plate becomes the leader of that problem. Not the most senior person. Not the most experienced. The one willing to do the thing everyone else is grateful someone else is doing.

When you step into that role, the room does not resist. It exhales. Because someone has taken responsibility, and that is exactly what most people wanted. The relief you feel when someone else decides? That is the same relief the room feels when you do it.

I built most of my career on this. I found the gaps between departments, the work that did not clearly belong to anyone, the problems that mattered and that everyone was hoping someone else would own. I bridged those gaps on my own, without a title for any of it. Eventually my company built a leadership role around my skill set because I had already been doing the work.

I also stopped asking permission to do what the business needed done. That is the moment my career shifted. Not the day I got a promotion. The day I stopped waiting for one.

The people I have watched get promoted faster than anyone expected are usually just the ones who stopped waiting. Their careers shifted the moment they decided to do the work the title was supposed to give them permission to do.

How to Do This Before You Have the Title

The mechanism is straightforward. The execution is harder, because most of us were taught to wait.

Wait for the promotion. Wait for the right moment. Wait until we feel ready.

We are almost never ready. The waiting is the problem.

Here is what choosing to lead actually looks like:

In a meeting that is circling: Name the decision. "It sounds like we are trying to decide between X and Y. What do we need to make that call?" You are not the most senior person in the room. You are the person who noticed what the meeting needed.

When someone brings you a problem with no direction: Have a point of view. "Here is my recommendation. Tell me what I am missing." You do not need authority to have an opinion worth hearing.

When something does not have clear ownership: Volunteer for the gap. Not to claim territory, but because someone needs to own it and you are willing to be that person.

When the decision is hard and nobody is moving: Be the first to put a stake in the ground. "Here is where I land on this. Push back if I am missing something." Perfection is not the bar. Direction is.

None of these require a title. They require the willingness to take the burden of the decision off someone else's plate.

One thing worth naming: many people with formal leadership titles are not doing this. They have the title and they are still avoiding the decision. They seek more consensus. They defer again.

The people around them know it. The room follows the person deciding, not the person with the biggest badge.

If you are the most junior person in the room and you are the only one willing to own the decision, the room will follow you. Not because you outranked anyone. Because you were the only one who would.

---

The Uncomfortable Part

A lot of people who hold leadership titles chose the title, not the leading. They wanted the compensation, the status, the org chart position. They did not want the accountability that comes with making the call. And it shows.

Not in their confidence, but in the circling. The rooms that never resolve. The decisions that keep getting pushed until the deadline makes them.

Choosing to lead means choosing the accountability too. Not just the upside. That is what most people are avoiding when they wait for permission.

You do not have to wait.

---

Monday Action

Pick one meeting this week where no one is making the call.

Before it ends, name the decision out loud. "It sounds like we need to decide X before we can move forward. What is missing for us to make that call today?"

You do not need to be the most senior person in the room. You need to be the most willing.
