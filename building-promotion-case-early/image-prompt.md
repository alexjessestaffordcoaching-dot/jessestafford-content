## Shared Elements

**Core situation:** A professional discovers that the people with real authority over their promotion knew nothing about their goals or contributions — the actual decision was already forming in rooms they were never part of, weeks before the formal conversation.

**Context box text:** THE DECISION HAPPENED BEFORE YOU ASKED

**Emotional tone:** Direct and focused. Jesse's expression is calm and clear-eyed — the look of someone who has figured out how the system actually works and is helping someone else see it. Not distressed. Not victorious. Just clear. This tone holds across all three concepts.

## Concept 1 — The System

**Visual metaphor:** A large organizational routing panel — like a telephone switchboard or pneumatic tube junction — mounted on the right side of the frame. Multiple active lines route through a central hub (the calibration/decision point). Jesse's single line connects only to one port and terminates before reaching the hub. The decision network runs without his input.

**First-second read:** Jesse's connection stops short of the hub. All the active lines flow past his line into the decision center. He is in the network but not connected to where the decisions route.

**Compositional approach:** Wide shot. The routing panel fills the right two-thirds of the frame. Jesse stands small in the lower-left, looking toward the hub. A single line traces from Jesse's position to one port — and ends there, visibly shorter than the other lines that continue into the active hub. The hub is the brightest, most detailed area of the panel. Jesse's line terminates in dead space.

Crisp, uniform charcoal ink outlines (#333333), medium line weight throughout. Comic book precision — not sterile flat-art, not heavy superhero cross-hatching. Lines should suggest a technical patent drawing or a well-crafted indie comic panel. Loose, organic watercolor fills — visibly hand-painted. Color intentionally bleeds slightly outside the ink lines or leaves small white gaps at boundaries. Wet-edge textures and transparency variations are required — these are features, not errors. No digital gradients. No glow effects. No perfectly aligned fills. Mechanical background elements suggest systems and structure without being literal or dominant. 16:9 aspect ratio, single panel.

The active hub lines are rendered in the primary blue wash. Jesse's single terminated line is charcoal — visually distinct from the active blue connections. Supporting warm tan or aged-paper tones may appear in the board's mechanical housing.

Standing slightly sideways, looking toward the routing panel hub — calm, focused, registering what he is seeing. Not reaching toward it. Not touching it. Just looking, with clarity.

One charcoal line traces from near his feet toward the board, reaching one of the lower ports — and stopping there. The port he is connected to does not route through to the hub. The mechanical background behind Jesse suggests a workspace with exposed pipes and gear elements, rendered lightly so it supports rather than competes.

Context box:
Uppercase sans-serif text inside a solid white (#ffffff) rectangle with a sharp charcoal (#333333) border. Placed at the bottom of the panel. Text: THE DECISION HAPPENED BEFORE YOU ASKED

## Concept 2 — The Room

**Scene dynamic:** Jesse sits across a small, informal table from a skip-level leader. The moment: Jesse has just said something that has landed as genuinely new information. The skip-level leader's expression is surprised — leaning back slightly, processing. Jesse is forward, composed, direct. The gap between them — the space across the table — is the information gap being bridged for the first time.

**First-second read:** One person surprised by what the other is telling them. The gap in the information between two people who should have been aligned. The surprise is the point.

**Compositional approach:** Medium shot, two people at a small table. The skip-level leader is positioned on the left, leaning back with raised eyebrows — surprised expression, clearly receiving unexpected information. Jesse is on the right, leaning slightly forward, calm and direct, in the middle of making something clear. The open space of the table surface between them is intentional — the information gap made physical. Equal character scale communicates a peer-level professional moment.

## Concept 3 — The Reframe

**Unexpected angle:** The absent element. Jesse arrives at the meeting room door to find the calibration meeting already over — empty chairs, a whiteboard with decisions still visible, coffee cups, and through a far window the last of the meeting participants dispersing. He arrived at the right room at the wrong time.

**First-second read:** Jesse at the threshold of a room full of empty chairs. The meeting is over. The decision has been made. Too late.

**Why it works:** The concept is communicated by what is missing — the people who decided his fate are gone. Jesse isn't being excluded in the moment; he simply wasn't there when it happened, and he didn't know to be. The empty room is more unsettling than a closed door.

A whiteboard on the far wall has visible writing and notes — too small to read, but clearly decision-relevant. Two or three coffee cups rest on the table, one with visible residual steam, indicating the meeting just ended. Through a narrow window or second doorway in the far background, very small human shapes (two or three figures) are dispersing or walking away — rendered small and peripheral. Jesse is positioned in the left-center of the frame, at the threshold, his body language registering the empty room — not panicked, but clearly processing what he is seeing.
