## Flags

### Story Frame
**Section:** The Four Ways to Start section
**Issue:** Three of the four techniques are written in pure instruction mode. "Teach what you know while you know it. Every process you run..." reads like a checklist from a leadership article, not like Jesse in a conversation. His Reddit voice gives instruction embedded inside a real observation: "Once you can do something quickly, delegate it." He would not deliver four items with bold headers unless each one had a line of grounding first.
**Suggestion:** After each bolded header, add one sentence of grounding from Jesse's observed experience before the instruction — something like "I learned this the hard way when..." or "The thing I kept doing was..." Not a full story each time. One orienting line that proves this came from somewhere real, not from a list he made up.

---

### Bar Test — Mechanism Section
**Section:** "Organizations are not built to promote people who cannot be replaced. They are built to promote people they can afford to move."
**Issue:** This is accurate but sounds like an org-design lecture. Jesse would not say "organizations are built to..." at a bar. He would say it as something he figured out or observed: "Every promotion decision I've been part of came down to one thing..." The current version frames the mechanism as structural fact rather than something Jesse learned from watching it happen.
**Suggestion:** Add one line of lived grounding before this observation. Something like "Here is what I figured out after watching this happen for fifteen years:" followed by the current text. Alternatively, reframe the observation as coming from Jesse's vantage point: "Every time I've sat in a promotion conversation, the real question underneath it was not whether this person was ready. It was whether we could afford to move them."

---

### Bar Test — "Most people carry the assumption..."
**Section:** "Most people carry the assumption that being indispensable is the goal."
**Issue:** "Carry the assumption" is slightly academic. Jesse in Reddit uses "most leaders I talk to" or simply "the assumption is." He names the belief directly rather than attributing it to what people carry.
**Suggestion:** Rewrite without "carry." Try: "The assumption most leaders operate under is that being indispensable is the goal." Or even shorter: "Everyone assumes that being indispensable is the goal." Direct, bar-level.

---

### Sentence Rhythm — Repeated "I Started" Pattern
**Section:** "I had to rebuild how I operated after that promotion. I started making sure my team was visible, not just me. I started sharing the context behind decisions, not just the decisions themselves. I started putting people into rooms they had never been in before so they could build relationships I had been building for them."
**Issue:** Three consecutive sentences open with "I started." The anaphora creates a list-feel rather than a narrative-feel. In Jesse's Reddit writing, repetition of this kind is more deliberate and shorter — he does not run three "I started" lines in a row. It sounds drafted rather than spoken.
**Suggestion:** Break the repetition after the second "I started" sentence. The third could open differently — "The goal was to make sure..." or lead with the outcome rather than the action.

---

### Quick Bridge — Meta Phrase
**Section:** "This post names the mechanism behind that trap, and gives you four specific moves for building your way out of it."
**Issue:** "This post names..." is meta-commentary about the post. The Quick Bridge should describe what the reader gets, not what the article does. Every other Quick Bridge example (from medium-strategy.md) describes the reader's situation and outcome directly without referring to "this post."
**Suggestion:** Rewrite the final sentence as the reader's outcome, not the article's function. Example: "...and here are four specific moves for building your way out of it." or frame it as: "...you can learn to make yourself replaceable in this role on purpose — so your organization can finally afford to promote you."

---

### Authority Language — One Instance
**Section:** "Your value in your current role exceeds the cost of developing your replacement."
**Issue:** This reads as an economic-analysis sentence rather than something observed from experience. It is abstract and technical. Jesse's Reddit voice does not sound like this.
**Suggestion:** Replace with an observation Jesse could have made from watching it happen. Something like: "Your organization has not developed a plan to replace you because they do not need to. You are doing the job." The mechanism is the same — the language is grounded.

---

### Bolding — Missing Third Takeaway
**Section:** Entire post
**Issue:** Only 2 bolded takeaways appear in the post body (the core insight in the opening, and the "if the honest answer is no" line). Brand-voice.md asks for 2-3 bolded key takeaways. A third is missing.
**Suggestion:** Bold the line "Training your replacement is not a nice-to-have. It is the actual work." in The Shift section. This is the post's second most important insight and currently sits un-bolded.

---

### Opening Specificity
**Section:** "When my responsibilities expanded and I got promoted to a bigger role, I thought I had figured it out."
**Issue:** "A bigger role" is vague. The story behind this is concrete (team of 6 grew to over 100 eventually). Jesse's Reddit voice always names the actual scale: "my team grew from 5 to 20 overnight." The current opening underplays the specificity that makes the story feel real.
**Suggestion:** Tighten the opening to name the actual situation without overstuffing it. Something like: "When I got promoted out of the team I had spent two years building, I assumed they would be fine." This removes the vague "bigger role" and keeps focus on what happened — he left the team. That is the actual problem.

The short punchy paragraphs following the promotion — "Nobody picked up the work... Nobody knew it mattered... Nobody knew it was happening at all... I had never shown them any of it." — this is exactly how Jesse talks when he is making a point. Short, accumulating, honest. Do not touch this.

**2. "Training your replacement is not a nice-to-have. It is the actual work."**
Perfect Jesse. Direct, declarative, memorable. Only issue is it is not bolded (flagged above). Sentence itself is strong.

**3. Monday Action**
"That conversation is the beginning of your next promotion." — The closing line of the Monday Action is the strongest sentence in the post. Specific, usable, punchy. Leave it exactly as is.
