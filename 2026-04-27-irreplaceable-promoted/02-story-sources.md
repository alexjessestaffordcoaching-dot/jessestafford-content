## Primary Source

**Story ID:** Story 3 — His Biggest Leadership Failure / Reddit Follow-up Comment
**Why it fits:** Jesse directly states the exact mechanism: "if you are irreplaceable to your team, you are also not promotable." This is the core insight of the post. He describes the failure (not training replacement), the consequence (team lost ground), and the lesson he learned.
**Source:** r/Leadership, August 2025
**Exact text:**

> The failure... I never shared, showed, or brought my team into what I was doing around them. I allowed them to sit at their desks and produce, while I did all the work of making sure other people knew how and why they were successful.
>
> A couple of years into this role, my responsibilities were drastically expanded, and I was promoted. I no longer have the room in my schedule to do everything I was doing for my original team and what my expanded team needed. This meant that what I had been doing for them just stopped happening. Nobody was there to pick it up. Nobody knew why it was important and didn't see a need to put in that work.
>
> The result was that the team fell into a mild obscurity for several years, while I worked to build up people with the skills and desire to do the work that made and showed their success.
>
> This is where I learned why delegation is important, why you should have a successor, and why shielding your team from the rest of the company is actually harmful to their success.

**Follow-up statement (same thread):**

> Haha, I am now several levels higher. That team in my story was six; my team is now over 100 and is growing. :) I've never forgotten those lessons and apply them every day. Here is the simple truth: **if you are irreplaceable to your team, you are also not promotable.** Learning that lesson also ignited my passion for strong leadership and led me to become an executive/leadership coach to help others become great leaders.

## Guiding Principle

**Principle:** Teams > Individuals

From guiding-principles.md:

> Products and businesses don't run on individuals. You have to have a team to be successful, and your team has to be successful for you to be successful. You cannot win without the people around you.
>
> Everyone you work with is your team — peers, your boss's peers, direct reports. If you focus only on your direct reports, you are operating like an individual.

This principle underlies the post: if you operate like an individual (making yourself indispensable, not building team capacity), you lock yourself into that role. The solution (building your replacement) comes from the Teams > Individuals principle.
