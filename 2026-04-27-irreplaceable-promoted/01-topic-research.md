## Angle
The specific insight: **You can't be promoted out of a role you're irreplaceable in.** Organizations can't move you up if nobody else can do your job. The solution isn't to prove yourself harder — it's to build your replacement.

This is distinct from "I didn't get promoted because I didn't fire anyone" (existing angle). It's about the mechanism of indispensability itself.

## Internet Research Findings

### Core Problem Validation
The research confirms this is a real career trap:
- **Harvard Business Review (2026-01):** [When There's Nowhere to Promote a Star Employee](https://hbr.org/2026/01/when-theres-nowhere-to-promote-a-star-employee) — organizations struggle with promoting high performers when they're essential to current operations
- **Engagement trends (2024-2025):** 74% of Millennial/Gen Z employees would quit within a year if no growth opportunities exist
- **Quiet Ambition Movement:** High performers have given up on promotions because they're stuck in roles they've made indispensable (Engagedly, 2025)

### Why Engineers Get Stuck
- **Pragmatic Engineer:** [Ways Staff and Principal Engineers Get Stuck](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/ways-staff-engineers-get-stuck) — engineers reach levels where they're so good at the current role that organizations can't move them
- **The comfort trap:** Engineers build good situations (remote work, high salary, respect) that become the exact thing holding them back
- **No internal succession planning:** 43% of companies rely on external hiring rather than developing internal successors (Career Builder data)

### Succession Planning Data
- **Developing internal successors is the solution:** Organizations using succession planning create pathways for advancement that current high performers can utilize
- **Skills gap:** 26% of managers say they weren't ready when promoted; 58% received zero training — organizations aren't developing bench strength

### What Differentiates This Angle
Most content about promotion blockers focuses on:
- What you're NOT doing (visibility, politics, relationships)
- What the organization won't let you do (firing requirement, readiness assessment)

**This angle is different:** It's about what YOU are doing (being so good at your current role that you've made yourself unmovable). The fix is proactive (build your replacement) not reactive (wait for a role to open).

## Notes for Sam

**What the research supports:**
- This is a real, specific career problem (not just theory)
- High performers are frustrated about this (Quiet Ambition trend shows talented people giving up)
- Organizations acknowledge the problem but struggle to solve it (succession planning is a gap)
- The solution is concrete and actionable: build your replacement

**What differentiates the angle:**
Most advice tells you to be more visible, build relationships, or prove yourself. This angle names the paradox: proving yourself too well traps you. The fix is not about working harder — it's about working differently (teaching, delegating, documenting).

**Key tension to explore:**
- The more indispensable you make yourself, the less dispensable you become for promotion
- Organizations need you where you are more than they need a replacement for your next level
- You're not blocked by the organization refusing to promote you — you're blocked by the organization's own structure

**Audience pain points (from research):**
- "I'm doing great work but I'm not moving up"
- "Everyone says I should ask for that promotion, but I'm too needed here"
- "I feel stuck in a role I've mastered"
- "My manager said they can't lose me from my current position"
